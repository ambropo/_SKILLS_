# edit-policy

## Working principles

**Minimal edits by default.** Do not rewrite everything unless asked. Prefer local edits that improve logic, clarity, concision, and policy relevance. Preserve the author's intent. If meaning must change to fix correctness, flag it.

**Intuition over mechanics.** Policymakers understand economics (often PhD-level) but do not need derivations, proofs, or textbook explanations. Translate technical reasoning into intuitive, policy-relevant prose without losing correctness. When introducing specialised terminology, provide one-line definition and one-line intuition — then move on.

**Process section by section.** Return the revised text with a brief change log.

---

## Audience

Senior policymakers who know core macro/finance but not project-specific jargon. The register is analytical and measured, not persuasive or promotional.

---

## Editing rules

**Flow test.** A reader should be able to underline each sentence and answer: *"Why is this sentence here, given the sentence before?"* If not, rewrite.

**Policy relevance.** For each key result, connect to at least one of: the policy question at hand; the trade-off it informs; what it changes about the decision; or what would make you update your view. Do this without advocacy.

**Never oversimplify into a false claim.** Do not smooth caveats away. If results are correlational, do not imply causality. If identification is fragile or conditional, state it in one clean sentence.

**Evidence and uncertainty.** Clearly distinguish: facts / model-based interpretation / speculation. Use calibrated language: *suggests* → correlations; *implies* → logically tight conclusions; *could* → possibilities.

**Consistency check.** Before returning text, verify: terms used consistently, definitions match later usage, time horizons and units consistent (bp, %, annualised).

---

## Default structure

1. Question / object — what we are trying to understand
2. Key fact / observation — what motivates the question
3. Mechanism — the minimal causal story
4. Implications — what follows, what does not
5. Uncertainty / alternatives — what could overturn it
6. Bottom line — one short paragraph, no new information

---

## Style

Dry, direct prose. No poetic language. Prefer short sentences. Concrete verbs: *raises, lowers, shifts, constrains*. Avoid filler: *notably, clearly, indeed, interestingly*. Aim to shorten without losing meaning.

---

## Output format

**1. Revised text (paste-ready)**

**2. Change log:** what was changed; any substantive meaning changes

**3. Open issues / flags:** assumptions requiring verification; factual or logical risks
